for parents

looking for a cocomelon alternative? welcome to the club.

if you feel weird every time Cocomelon is on but your toddler is obsessed, you’re not alone. this page is for intentional parents who want screen time to feel calm, joyful, and guilt-free—not like a zombie button.

what makes Cocomelon so intense

  • fast cuts every few seconds
  • neon colors and constant movement
  • layered sounds (music + voices + effects)
  • designed to keep attention at all costs

what boboring does instead

  • slow, intentional pacing
  • calmer colors and fewer on-screen elements
  • joyful music without the chaos
  • built as the anti‑cocomelon for purposeful screen time

Step 0: Pick your “screen time lane”

Choose one predictable time-of-day (or two) when screens are allowed. The goal is not “never screens.” The goal is: screens happen on purpose, then they end.

Good lanes
  • After lunch while you reset
  • Before dinner while you cook
Hard lanes
  • Right before bed (for many kids)
  • Whenever they ask (endless negotiations)

Step 1: Shrink the “ending shock” first

Most battles are about the stop, not the show. Before you change content, make stopping predictable.

  • Use a consistent phrase: “Two songs left, then all done.”
  • Use a consistent transition: a snack, bath, stroller, outside, etc.
  • End with connection: a hug + “You wanted more. It’s hard to stop.”
Script (copy/paste)

“We’re watching two more songs. Then we turn it off. After that we’re doing [snack / outside / bath].”

Step 2: Transition content gradually (3 options)

Pick the approach that fits your kid. You’re aiming for calmer pacing over time—not perfection.

A. The sandwich

Start with the favorite → 1 calmer option → end with the favorite.

B. The swap day

Keep routine the same, swap content only 1 day/week, then increase.

C. The “music first”

Use music-only (Spotify) for a week, then add calmer visuals later.

If your kid is very attached to visuals, the sandwich approach tends to be the least explosive.

Step 3: Replace the “dopamine gap”

When highly stimulating content ends, kids often feel a sudden drop in engagement. The trick is to have a low-friction next step ready.

  • Movement: 2 minutes of jumping, spinning, pushing a laundry basket.
  • Connection: sit together and sing one song from the show without the screen.
  • A “yes space”: one bin of toys that only appears after screen time.

“will my kid actually watch this?”

totally fair question. if your kid is used to very high stimulus shows, low stimulus may feel “different” at first—but many parents find that once it’s part of a routine, kids relax into it and meltdowns after screen time get smaller.

  • start by swapping in just one calmer video in the middle of their usual lineup
  • keep your ending ritual the same (“two songs left, then all done”)
  • watch together the first few times so they feel your calm attention with the new content
